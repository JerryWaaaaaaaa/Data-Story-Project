Introduction

Let’s start the conversation with this.

In both the East and the West, it is common to regard education as a tool to help find a good job. People believe that education can change their destiny and become a channel for upward movement of different social classes.

However, the turbulent job market and the rising tuition don’t say so. As a college student, the mismatch of the invest (the college education) and the return (a good job) makes me doubt if this common notion on education is even right. So I made some research and visualized some data to figure out if college education has ever been “a channel for upward movement of different social classes”.

Theories

There are several theories on the causes of tuition rises. I find the following two really intriguing.

The Bennett Hypothesis

In 1987, then-Education Secretary Willian J. Bennett made an argument that federal loan is a main cause of the tuition rise. In his work Our Greedy Colleges, he said:

If anything, increases in financial aid in recent years have enabled colleges and universities blithely to raise their tuitions, confident that Federal loan subsidies would help cushion the increase … Federal student aid policies do not cause college price inflation, but there is little doubt that they help make it possible.

So, how much has the student loan changed in the past 20 years?

➡️

It seems like there is no such positive correlation between tuition rise and student loan. The tuition rise is more rapid than the rise in student loans. Thus, instead of saying that the rise in student loan causes tuition rise, it's more reasonable to argue that the drastic rise in tuition keeps low-income-family students from college education resources as well as forces more students to look for more alternatives for financial aids and loans. For maintaining the equality in education, the government had to then provide more grants to the students to cover their bill. Meanwhile, some businesses provided scholarships to students while others saw this as a new business and started to provide loans to students.

What's fun is that later in 2013, Bennet revised his hypothesis as follows:

If the federal government gives money, tuition goes up. If the federal government doesn’t give money, it goes up … Federal student aid makes it easier for colleges to do what they’re going to do anyway, which is raise tuition.

Baumol's Cost Disease

This is a complicated one, it’s the rise of salaries in jobs that have experienced no or low increase of labor productivity, in response to rising salaries in other jobs that have experienced higher labor productivity growth. Simply it means, people doing jobs that can't be measured by labor productivity are paid more based on the years they have done the job.

When it comes to education, it means college has to pay higher salaries year by year. It’s reasonable for the students to pay more tuition to cover the cost, as they are the direct beneficiary.

Job Market

Some college students will decide to go for a post-graduate education, while the others will enter the job market. For those in the job market, they will encounter three different scenarios: getting a dream job, getting underemployed, or get unemployed.

To examine if college education has helped us find a better job, let's see the performance in four labor types: all workers, young workers, all college graduates, recent college graduates.

Unemployment

“Unemployment” occuers when workers who want to work are not able to find a job. Let's take a look at the unemployment rate of the four labor types.

➡️

The overall trend for each labor type are very similar to each other. Obviously, getting a college education will not prevent you from the historical trend of unemployment. What college education provides is, when job cut happens, college graduates seem to be lessly to get cut. Thus, college graduates seemingly secure their job better than those without a tertiary education.
